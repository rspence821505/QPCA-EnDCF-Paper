\subsection{``Controlled by kappa rather than d'' phrasing stronger than the stated variance bound}
\label{issue:kappa_vs_d_scope}
\severity{M}

\noindent\textbf{Status:} Potential overstatement (not an algebraic error, but the text reads stronger than what Theorem~4 literally provides without additional effective-dimension assumptions).

\noindent\textbf{Locations:}
Abstract (line 68), Remark ``Scope of the i.i.d.\ assumption'' (line 716), Conclusion (line 2168) in \texttt{uq\_paper\_draft.tex}.

\noindent\textbf{Statements in paper (representative):}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item Abstract: ``\ldots scales with retained rank rather than observation dimension.''
\item Remark ``Scope of the i.i.d.\ assumption'': ``\ldots sampling variance scales as $\mathcal{O}(1/N)$ and is controlled by $\kappa$ rather than $d$ \ldots''
\item Conclusion: ``\ldots sampling variance scales with retained spectral rank ($\kappa$) rather than observation dimension ($d$) \ldots''
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{Issue:}
Theorem~4's variance bound contains terms of order $N^{-1}$ that involve $\mathrm{tr}(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_E^{\,s})$ (and not a truncated trace such as $\mathrm{tr}(\mathbf{P}_\kappa^{\,s}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_E^{\,s})$). In general, $\mathrm{tr}(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_E^{\,s})$ can scale with the observation dimension $d$ unless one assumes an effective-dimension structure (e.g., spectral decay or normalization ensuring $\sum_{i=1}^{\kappa}\lambda_i=\mathcal{O}(\kappa)$ and that the dominant contributions are confined to the retained subspace). The \emph{projector-estimation/stability} contribution is explicitly $\kappa$-dependent (via $\kappa/\delta_\kappa^2$), but the paper's phrasing can be read as claiming the entire variance is generically $\kappa$-controlled.

\noindent\textbf{Proposed fix:}
Qualify the rank-vs-dimension statements to match what is proved:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item Replace ``controlled by $\kappa$ rather than $d$'' with: ``$\mathrm{Var}=\mathcal{O}(1/N)$ with $\kappa$-dependent prefactors arising through projector stability (via $\kappa/\delta_\kappa^2$); in effective-dimension regimes, the dominant sampling-sensitive contribution behaves like $\mathcal{O}(\kappa/N)$.''%
\item Alternatively, add an explicit effective-dimension assumption (e.g., $\mathrm{tr}(\mathbf{P}_\kappa^{\,s}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_E^{\,s})\lesssim \kappa$ and replace $\mathrm{tr}(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_E^{\,s})$ by a truncated trace in the narrative where appropriate).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Corollary on stochastic perturbations assumes deterministic gain but the baseline stochastic EnKF gain is empirical}
\label{issue:corollary2_scope_mismatch}
\severity{M}

\noindent\textbf{Status:} Scope mismatch (the corollary is correct as written under its assumptions, but its relationship to the empirical-gain stochastic baselines can be misread).

\noindent\textbf{Locations:}
Corollary ``Comparison to stochastic EnKF observation perturbations'' (line 1435) vs.\ baseline algorithm definition (Appendix algorithm, e.g.\ lines 2240--2250) in \texttt{uq\_paper\_draft.tex}.

\noindent\textbf{Issue:}
The corollary explicitly assumes ``$\mathbf{K}^{(w)}$ is deterministic'' (line 1435). However, in the stochastic EnKF baselines implemented/defined in the paper, $\mathbf{K}^{(w)}$ is computed from sample covariances (hence is random across repeated ensemble draws/trials). The corollary therefore compares QPCA-EnDCF to a stylized stochastic-EnKF-with-deterministic-gain setting unless the conditioning/averaging over $\mathbf{K}^{(w)}$ is stated explicitly.

\noindent\textbf{Proposed fix:}
Keep the deterministic-gain statement if desired, but add one clarifying sentence (in the corollary or its remark) such as:
\begin{quote}
``In empirical implementations, $\mathbf{K}^{(w)}$ is random; the covariance identity holds conditionally on $\mathbf{K}^{(w)}$ (or on the forecast ensemble), and the unconditional injected-variance term becomes $\frac{1}{N}\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{K}^{(w)}\mathbf{R}^{(L)}(\mathbf{K}^{(w)})^\top]$.''%
\end{quote}
Alternatively, restate the covariance identity directly in conditional or unconditional averaged form.

\subsection{``Constants independent of N'' phrasing vs.\ dependence on the empirical gain}
\label{issue:constants_independent_of_N}
\severity{L}

\noindent\textbf{Status:} Presentation ambiguity (not necessarily incorrect, but can be read as inconsistent because $\mathbf{K}^{\mathrm{DC}}$ is an empirical, $N$-dependent object).

\noindent\textbf{Location:}
Corollary on truncation-level selection, line 1519 in \texttt{uq\_paper\_draft.tex}.

\noindent\textbf{Issue:}
The text states that constants are independent of $N$ but ``depend only on $\|\mathbf{K}^{\mathrm{DC}}\|_2$ and $\|\mathbf{R}^{(L)}\|_2$''. Since $\mathbf{K}^{\mathrm{DC}}$ is computed from a finite ensemble, $\|\mathbf{K}^{\mathrm{DC}}\|_2$ is random and typically depends on $N$. The intended meaning is presumably that the constants depend on deterministic \emph{moment bounds} for the gain (e.g., the gain-moment assumption in the theory section) or on a uniform stability bound from a stabilized inverse, rather than on a realized sample gain.

\noindent\textbf{Proposed fix:}
Replace ``depend only on $\|\mathbf{K}^{\mathrm{DC}}\|_2$'' by a deterministic control such as ``depend only on $\mathbb{E}\|\mathbf{K}^{\mathrm{DC}}\|_2^2$ (gain-moment assumption) and $\|\mathbf{R}^{(L)}\|_2$'' (or on an almost-sure bound $\|\mathbf{K}^{\mathrm{DC}}\|_2\le K_{\max}$ when a stabilized inverse is used), and then state explicitly that these bounds are assumed uniformly in $N$.
